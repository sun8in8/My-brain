## Script

### Opening — the letter (0:00–2:30)

*[Direction: Silence first. Then: pen on paper. Slow, deliberate writing. 25–30 seconds before voice. The writing continues under the voice throughout this section.]*

**[Whisper]**
This is a letter I wrote but never gave anyone.

*(pause)*

I found it recently, at the back of a drawer. I don't know exactly when I wrote it — somewhere in the middle of a year that was harder than it looked from the outside. The handwriting is messier than usual. I think I wrote it at night.

*[Direction: Pen slows. A pause. Then continues.]*

**[Whisper]**
I'm going to read it to you tonight.

*(softly)*

Not because it's remarkable. But because I suspect some part of it might be a letter you've written too, in some form, and never sent either.

*(a long pause)*

I'll change a few things. The names. Some of the details that are too specific to be anyone else's story. But the feeling — I'll leave that exactly as it was.

---

### Act one — reading the letter (2:30–9:00)

*[Direction: Soft paper sounds — the letter being unfolded. Then: slow, deliberate reading pace. More pauses than feel natural. Let each thought land.]*

**[Soft-spoken]**
*Dear —*

*(pause)*

*I've started this letter four times now. Each time I get to the second paragraph and I stop, because I can't work out if I'm writing this for you or for me. I think maybe that's the most honest version of why I never sent it. I couldn't tell who needed to hear it more.*

*(gently)*

*I want to say: I think I got some things wrong. Not in a dramatic way — not the kind of wrong that makes a good story. Just in the small, quiet way that accumulates over time without you noticing until one day you're standing in a room and you realise everything in it looks slightly different than you thought it did.*

*[Direction: A pause. Paper settles. Pen picks up again — as if adding a note in the margin.]*

**[Soft-spoken]**
*I've been thinking about the evening we argued about something I can't even remember now. Isn't that strange? The thing itself has completely gone. But I remember the feeling. The particular quality of the silence afterwards. The way we both went to bed pretending we were fine when neither of us was fine at all.*

*(pause)*

*I think about that silence a lot. What was in it. What we were both choosing not to say. I used to think silence like that was a failure. Now I think it might have been the most we were capable of that night, and maybe that deserves a little more kindness than I gave it.*

*[Direction: Long pause. Rain arrives softly in the background.]*

**[Soft-spoken]**
*I'm not writing this to reopen anything. I'm writing it because I've been carrying it around and I would like to put it down.*

*(very softly)*

*I think you were doing the best you could. I think I was too, most of the time. I think that's true of most people, most of the time, and we spend so much energy not believing it.*

---

### Act two — what the letter taught me (9:00–17:00)

*[Direction: Letter is folded back. Long pause. Pen on paper — new writing now, slower. ~45 seconds before voice.]*

**[Soft-spoken]**
I've never read that out loud before.

*(pause)*

It's interesting — the things that sound different spoken. Some of it lands harder. Some of it sounds softer than it did on the page. A few lines I thought were quite wise now sound a little like someone who was just sad and trying to make the sadness mean something.

*(a small, warm sound)*

Which is also allowed.

*[Direction: Soft page turning — as if looking back through the notebook where the letter was kept.]*

**[Soft-spoken]**
There's a reason we write letters we never send. It isn't really about the other person. Or it starts with them, but it becomes something else.

It becomes a way of understanding what you actually think. Because thinking happens in fog. But writing has to choose words. And words, when you make yourself pick them carefully, have a way of showing you the shape of something you couldn't quite see before.

*(gently)*

I've done this with a lot of things I couldn't say out loud. Arguments I was in the middle of. Grief I didn't know how to hold. Conversations I needed to have with myself about what I actually wanted.

*(pause)*

I never sent most of them. Some I burned, a little dramatically, which I don't regret. A few I kept. And one or two I did eventually send — months later, rewritten, with the fog cleared out of them.

*[Direction: Rain softens. Long page-turning sequence. ~90 seconds. Unhurried.]*

**[Whisper]**
I think the ones I kept — like this one — I kept because they remind me of who I was at a particular moment. And I've realised I don't want to lose those versions of myself entirely, even the ones that were confused, or sad, or writing messy letters at night that they didn't know how to finish.

*(pause)*

They were trying. That counts for something.

*(softly)*

You're trying too. I can tell, because you're here. Because you came somewhere quiet at the end of a day that probably asked a lot of you. Because some part of you knows you need more than noise.

That's not nothing. That's actually quite a lot.

---

### Wind-down — put the letter down (17:00–22:00)

*[Direction: Sound of paper being folded slowly. Then: placed in a drawer. Soft close. Rain is still present, very gently.]*

**[Whisper]**
I'm going to put the letter away again.

*(warmly)*

Not because it needs to be hidden. Just because it's said what it needed to say. Some things you carry until you don't need to carry them anymore. And then you put them down — not as a big moment, not with ceremony — just quietly, when you're ready.

*(pause)*

If there's something you've been carrying — a letter you haven't written, or one you have written and folded up and put in a drawer — I want you to know that it doesn't have to be resolved tonight. You don't have to have the hard conversation or reach the understanding or find the words.

*(very softly)*

Tonight you just have to rest.

*[Direction: Rain very soft now. Almost gone.]*

**[Whisper]**
You're allowed to put things down for the evening.

You can pick them back up tomorrow, or next week, or when you're ready. They'll still be there.

*(a long pause)*

But right now — let them rest.

*(barely audible)*

Goodnight.

*[Direction: Silence except for the last traces of rain. Fade over 2–3 minutes.]*
